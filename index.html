Revisiting the “hardest” places to “live” in the U.S.

Andrea J. Allen, Aura M. Alonso Rodriguez, Mariah C. Boudreau, Ben E. Camber, Mahalia B. Clark, Alexander Daniels, Alison E. Hall,

  1. INTRODUCTION

In 2014, the Upshot data science team of the New York Times compiled six data points on every county in the United States of America (US) to answer the question “Where are the hardest places to live in the U.S.?” The size data points in question were: education, median household income, unemployment rate, disability rate, life expectancy and obesity.

As we will discuss, there are no perfect Quality of Life metrics; and the six data points considered in the Upshot piece come with several caveats. For example, education is determined as the percentage of residents with at least a bachelor’s degree, without any control for the average age of residents, many of whom in some cases can be too young to even hold a bachelor’s degree. Disability rate is not a rate per se, but rather the difference between the percentage of the population collecting federal disability benefits and those collecting Social Security retirement benefits.

With these metrics, the Upshot then produced a global ranking of counties by ranking the combined ranks of each county in all six categories. Upon recreating the same map as the Upshot article, it was found out that each of the six data points incorporated in the ranking system were weighted equally. While this is a good strategy for a simple analysis, it was thought to be more beneficial if these metrics could be weighted differently and according to one’s preferences. First because many of the original metrics are heavily correlated such that putting equal weight to education and median salary, for example, is virtually equivalent to double counting the same metric. Second because having a customizable map allows the user to make their own definition of “where the hardest place to live is” in their eyes.

Beyond the metrics, the visualisation from the Upshot also had some important issues. The map was colored using a diverging scheme: Blue counties do better than the median county shown in white while orange counties do worse. Bluer counties rank better, and redder counties rank worse. Beyond the obvious danger of using a color scheme close to the classic blue/red divide of the American political spectrum, the diverging scheme also creates a false divide by putting a lot of weight on the mid-ranked counties. Counties close to the middle tend to be very similar, yet those a few ranks above the median are shown as “better” while those a few ranks below are shown as “worse”. Diverging color schemes are useful when there is a middle value of importance (e.g., zero, distinguishing positive and negative quantities) not for a continuous variable like rank.

In what follows, we look at the existing literature on migration patterns in the US and ask to what extent people can and do choose where to live. We then revisit the original Quality of Life data used by the Upshot, modify their visualisation and include additional metrics. Our updated interactive visualisation can be found below with important context and details thereafter. This work allows us to delve further in what quantitative spatial data can tell us about quality of life in the US.

  1. MIGRATION PATTERNS

Patterns of interstate or intercounty migration in the United States is another significant yet unacknowledged feature in the original presentation. Populations are dynamic, and trends or patterns in its geographical distribution have important effects on per capita state income levels [1], state expenditures [2], local economies [3],  and age distribution [2] to name a few - especially in the United States where internal migration rates are historically among the largest compared to other nations [4]. Specifically, 3% of the population has been observed to move across state lines in any given year, with about 10% of the population changing states within a five year period [5]. It follows that rates of unemployment, income levels, and several other factors included in the NYT quality of life metric are greatly impacted by levels of migration rates across states. In this paper, we include correlations between migration rates between counties and quality of life factors included in the NYT model. Our results demonstrate what we expect: the above factors are highly correlated with migration rates between counties.

Notable work on internal migration in the US as a subject of study began around the 1960’s when scientists and economists investigated the causes and effects of migration on localities [3]. The first models of migration cast migration as an “equilibrating mechanism” [6] which included the early gravity-type models which assumed migration rates as proportional to the size of the origin or destination population but inversely proportional to geographical distance [7] while other models proposed that the main cause of migration is the striving of individuals to maximize their expected benefits such as  difference in wages [8] or general return on labor skillset as a function of physical location [9,4]. Together, the formulations and assumptions of the early models form prototypes of much of the future work, such as the work of Plane [10] and Greenwood [11] among others [4, 5, 12, 13]. Modern work ranges from linking climate change to out-migration in the Corn Belt among young adults [14] to including indicators of state expenditure policy such as the number of seats in the state’s legislative chamber [15] - to name a few. While a thorough review of recent work is outside of the scope of this paper, the account provided here demonstrates the great variety and thought applied to the understanding and formulation of internal migration and its effects on local life in the US.

People choose where to move based on a wide variety of factors in addition to quality of life. Employment is a major consideration, and many people move to pursue new job opportunities. Family ties are another important factor, as people often choose to move or stay put in order to be close to relatives. But people’s connection to a landscape and place itself should not be ignored. In a 2014 survey of Vermonters who either left or stayed in the state, attachment to Vermont’s natural landscape outranked even family ties as a motivation for staying [16]. This may be a more important factor in scenic rural areas, but it shows that landscape should not be discounted as a component of people’s quality of life. Research has shown that there is a small but significant relationship between net migration among rural counties and features of the natural landscape such as topography, water bodies, and the amount of forest. People are generally attracted to areas with more varied topography, such as hills and mountains, as well as to coastal areas and those with plentiful lakes. Finally, people seem to prefer areas with an intermediate amount of forest interspersed with open land.

Climate can also influence where people prefer to settle, and even where it is safe to do so. Over past decades, people have been moving to areas with warmer, sunnier winters and more temperate summers, in particular to the West and South. However, these pleasant features of the past and present climate may have lured people to areas that are more at risk from climate change. While the western United States offers warm sunny winters, scenic mountains, and mild coastal areas, it has suffered in recent years from increasingly devastating wildfires that have burned millions of acres, costing dozens of lives, destroying homes, and doing billions of dollars worth of damage each year. These fires are linked to warmer, dryer conditions due to climate change, and are only expected to get worse in future.

Meanwhile in the Southeast, people have long enjoyed warm winters and access to the ocean, but people living along the Southeast and Gulf coasts are at increasing risk from more intense hurricanes and sea-level rise. As climate change warms the Earth, it also warms the oceans, and more water evaporates making for a more humid atmosphere. All of these factors create conditions that favor more powerful storms. We have already seen an uptick in the number of more intense category 4 and 5 hurricanes in the last few years, and this trend of stronger hurricanes is also likely to continue moving forward.  These storms do sudden and devastating damage, but coastal cities such as New Orleans and Miami are also vulnerable to the creeping threat of sea-level rise. Finally, both the Southeast and the Southwest have been seeing rising summer temperatures, leading to more frequent and dangerous heat waves. This has contributed to a notable increase in the heat-related deaths, which account for hundreds of deaths a year across the United States.

In our updated visualization, we therefore consider many of the factors noted as causes of migration while potentially adding features which previously have been ignored in these models such as weather variables.

  1. STATISTICAL ANALYSES

3.1 Correlation Analyses

To explore the Upshot data, we first evaluated the relationship between the Quality of Life metrics included in the dataset per county in the United States. We used the GGally package in R to build a correlation matrix composed by linear regressions between each pair of metrics on the left, the Spearman's rank-order correlation coefficients on the right, and variable distributions on the diagonal (Figure 1). This demonstrated that all metrics are significantly correlated, and some show very strong correlations (Spearman coefficient higher than 0.6) such as Disability and Income, and Life Expectancy and Education.

Figure 1. Correlation matrix of relationships between each pair of Quality of Life metrics.

We can also explore the relationship between these variables and net migration between counties (Figure 2, left panel). Net migration (people moving to a county - people leaving a county in a given year) data were obtained for US counties for 2016, and compared to the Upshot’s Quality of Life metrics. Net migration had correlation coefficients ranging from -0.26 to 0.24 with these variables. We also compared net migration with a range of landscape and climate features, as a way to investigate the impact of nature and climate on people’s quality of life and what might draw them to move to a new place (Figure 2, right panel). Net migration had correlation coefficients ranging from -0.031 to 0.12 with these variables, which included mean January temperatures, a measure of January sun, mean July temperatures, mean July humidity, a topography code indicating more hilly or mountainous terrain, and a metric for surface water incorporating the area of lakes and coastal waters for each US county.

Figure 2. Correlation matrices showing relationships between (left panel) quality of life metrics with county’s net migration scaled to county population size, and (right panel) landscape and climate metrics with net migration. Jan Temp: Mean January Temperature, Jan Sunshine: Mean January Sunshine days, July Temp: Mean July Temperature, July Humidity: Mean July Humidity, Topographic Var.: indicator of topographic variation (more hilly or mountainous), In Water Area: natural log of a county’s surface water area.

3.2 Regression Analysis

To expand on the correlation analysis, linear regression analyses were run between net migration and each of the Upshot and the environmental variables (Table 1). Most of the variables had very small but significant relationships with net migration, with p-values below 0.0005 and R2 values ranging from 0.027 to 0.056 for the Upshot variables and from 0.005 to 0.019 for the environmental variables. The exceptions were for regressions with unemployment (p = 0.03), January sun (p > 0.05), and July humidity (p > 0.05). This implies that the Upshot’s quality of life metrics may in fact be related to where people choose to live, or at least to where they moved in 2016. However, it’s also possible that these low p-values are an artifact of the large sample size, with 3000+ counties.

Using linear regression to understand the relationship between move distance and income, we receive a Pearson Correlation Coefficient of 0.2004. This means that our predictor, Move Distance, explains 4% of the variance observed in Median Income of County A. Furthermore, we obtain a regression coefficient of 181.6998, which means that we expect Median Income of Origin County to increase by about $181.70 (on average) for every unit in Move Distance. Finally, we get a p-value of zero, meaning that there is essentially a 0% chance that our observed slope is due to randomness in our data (we reject our null hypothesis of zero slope).

  1. CONCLUSION

In nature, individuals in rapidly changing environments have to make life-or-death decisions about where to live. Unfortunately, without perfect knowledge of their environment, individuals often end up in seemingly suitable habitats that actually cannot support them. For animals, these “ecological traps” are often caused by human activity, yet their implications in human society are largely unexamined.

Unfortunately, habitat traps are also already common in humans. People who can’t afford a mortgage are forced to rent at higher monthly rates. Unscrupulous financial institutions grant subprime mortgages, ticking time bombs for naive homebuyers, then make money on home foreclosures. Nineteenth century efforts to get homesteaders to the American West relied on largely fictitious descriptions of lands of milk and honey that never mentioned the droughts or locust swarms. Habitat traps that are either natural in origin or large enough in scale can be considered ecological, because they are not entirely under human control.  

Per our results, we know that human habitat decisions can’t be generalized to rely on a single abstract variable, be it median income or a figure that accounts for cost of living as well. The opportunity presented by a county depends completely on the individual deciding whether or not to relocate there. However, from our migration analysis, we see what variables are associated with the decision.

Per Table 1, obesity difference was the factor most strongly associated with net migration. This could mean humans are aware of access to food resources and stay away from food deserts that lead to obesity. Education difference was second most significantly associated with net migration, suggesting greater opportunity in places with more educated folks.

However,  a more likely scenario is that obesity and lack of education are further symptoms of the habitat traps that are really driving some people away from certain counties. Future work should therefore look not only at what quality of life metrics act as drivers of migration flows, but also at how knowledge of these metrics and of associated habitat traps feeds back on future migration. Otherwise, mapping quality of life is bound to only provide flawed, static and coarse-grained pictures of a complex dynamical system.
